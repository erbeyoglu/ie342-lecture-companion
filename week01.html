Can a linear program say that? Slides 4–9

Futoshiki and sudoku (slides 4–5) look nothing like the LPs of IE201 — but why exactly? For each requirement below, decide: can it be written with linear constraints on continuous variables, or does it need more? (Quiz items written for this demo; the slides pose the general question.)

correct so far: 0 / 0

Answer all six to see the moral of the story.

A linear constraint is a weighted sum of decision variables compared to a constant — nothing more. Everything a plain LP cannot say (whole numbers, either–or choices, “exactly one of”, products of variables) is precisely what the exact methods of this course will add.
spoiler — open after the class has answered LP-expressible: items 1 and 5 (the blend ratio linearizes: multiply by the nonnegative total to get 2a − b ≥ 0 — Example 8 on slide 32 uses exactly this trick). Not LP-expressible: “exactly one of {1,2,3,4}”, integrality, price × quantity, and either–or — they need integer variables or nonlinear terms. That is why sudoku resists IE201 tools, and why integer models (slide 9) are the subject of this course.

The shaded region is where the intended segmentation holds: each boundary line is one customer group changing its mind. Prices, not productions, are the decision variables here — LP does not care.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Optimum (computed from the slide’s model — the slide shows only the formulation): p₁ = $10, p₂ = $13, revenue = $29,500. Why not p₂ = $15? Because at (10, 15) group 2’s iPad surplus (0) drops below its iPhone surplus (2) — they defect, so p₂ − p₁ ≤ 3 binds together with p₁ ≤ 10. Apple leaves $2 of “incentive money” on the table for every group-2 customer to keep them on the iPad.

IP teaser. Give the model a binary switch b₁ ∈ {0, 1} meaning “security 1 is filled to its cap”. Which pair of linear constraints encodes “don’t touch S₂ before S₁ is full”? (preview of the integer tools this course builds; written for this demo)

Slide 22 asks two questions: are the constraints S₁+S₂+S₃ ≤ 10, S₁ ≤ 3, S₂ ≤ 5 enough — and what if the yields were 4%, 7%, 9%?
spoiler — open after the class has answered With yields 9/7/4 the LP optimum is (3, 5, 2) → $0.70M: greed fills the best security first, so the answer happens to respect the firm’s rule — the constraints are enough only by luck. Flip the yields to 4/7/9 and the same constraints let the LP dump all $10M into security 3 (→ $0.90M), while a rule-obeying investor is stuck at (3, 5, 2) → $0.65M. The priority rule (“don’t touch 2 before 1 is full”) is a logical condition that no linear inequality expresses — it needs the integer-programming machinery this course builds.
