The LP relaxation drops the integrality restriction, so its region contains every integer-feasible point — that is why, in a max problem, zLP* can never be below z* (slide 8). Rounding the LP answer down keeps you feasible here, but is it optimal?
spoiler — open after the class has answered LP relaxation: (x₁, x₂) = (10/7, 30/7) ≈ (1.43, 4.29) with zLP* = 112.86 (slide 7). Rounding down gives (1, 4) with z = 99 (slide 8) — feasible but not optimal. The IP optimum sits at (0, 5) with z* = 100 (slide 9), and it is not even a neighbor of the LP solution. Rounding can miss the true optimum — and in other instances it can even be infeasible.

Markers after reveal: Σ = the max-total assignment, min = the max-min assignment. Slide 29's trick for a forbidden pairing (faculty 4 can't teach course 7) is x₄₇ = 0 — or set p₄₇ = −M and let the objective do the banning. Slide 33 builds max-min with one extra variable: max d subject to d ≤ Σj pijxij for every faculty i.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Max-total: F1→C3, F2→C2, F3→C4, F4→C1 for a total of 29 — three faculty score a 9, and faculty 4 is stuck with a 2. Max-min: F1→C2, F2→C1, F3→C4, F4→C3 lifts the worst-off to 6 while the total only drops to 28. Fairness cost one point here — it can cost more. And slide 27's teaser: the assignment problem is the easy one — its LP relaxation already lands on integer corners, while the innocent-looking knapsack has no such luck.
