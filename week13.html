Sunco Oil — how many barrels per hour can reach the sink? Slides 4–6

Oil is pumped from so to si through pipelines of limited capacity: (so,1)=2, (so,2)=3, (1,2)=3, (1,3)=4, (3,si)=1, (2,si)=2. Each arc label reads flow / capacity. Click nodes to trace a path from so to si — when you reach si, the path automatically carries as much oil as its tightest pipe allows. You may also step backward along an arc that already carries flow (a residual move) to re-route oil. How much can you ship in total?

Slides 5–6 turn this hunt into an LP: let xij be the barrels per hour on arc (i,j), add an artificial return arc (si,so) of infinite capacity, and maximise xsi,so subject to 0 ≤ xij ≤ capacity on every arc and flow-in = flow-out at every node. Your clicking obeys exactly those two feasibility rules.
spoiler — open after the class has answered The maximum is Z = 3 barrels per hour (slide 6). One optimal solution: xso,1=2, xso,2=1, x12=1, x13=1, x3,si=1, x2,si=2 — so xsi,so=3. Watch out for the greedy trap: pushing 2 units along so-1-2-si first blocks every forward path, and only the backward hop 2→1 (Ford–Fulkerson's residual arc) frees the last unit.

Ford–Fulkerson on Sunco's network — you call the bottleneck Slides 7–12

The residual network keeps two numbers per pipeline: near the arc's tail, the remaining forward capacity; near its head, the backward (undo) capacity, shown in violet once it is positive. Each iteration the slides announce an augmenting path; your job is to read the residuals along it and click the bottleneck c*. The residual update then replays slides 10–11 exactly.

total flow: 0

Slides 8–9: find an augmenting path (source → sink, every arc with positive residual capacity), push its minimum residual capacity c*, decrease forward residuals by c*, increase backward residuals by c*, repeat until no augmenting path exists.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Iteration 1: so-1-2-si, c* = min{2,3,2} = 2 (slide 10). Iteration 2: so-2-1-3-si, c* = min{3,2,4,1} = 1 — its hop 2→1 rides the backward residual of arc (1,2), re-routing one earlier unit through 1→3→si (slide 11). Then no augmenting path remains: maximum flow = 2+1 = 3. Subtracting forward residuals from capacities gives the flows of slide 12: so→1: 2, so→2: 1, 1→2: 1, 1→3: 1, 3→si: 1, 2→si: 2.

Cut the pipelines — how cheap can a cut get? Slide 13

A cut splits the nodes into a source side S (containing so) and a sink side (containing si); its capacity is the total capacity of the arcs going from S to the sink side — arcs pointing back into S do not count (they are drawn dashed). Every cut's capacity is an upper bound on the flow. Click nodes 1, 2, 3 to move them between the sides and log the cuts you find. What is the cheapest cut — and what does it equal?

Slide 13 draws five example cuts on this network and states the punchline of the week: the maximum s–t flow equals the minimum s–t cut capacity.
spoiler — open after the class has answered The slide's five cuts: S={so} → 2+3 = 5; S={so,1} → 3+3+4 = 10; S={so,1,2} → 4+2 = 6; S={so,1,3} → 3+3+1 = 7; and S={so,1,2,3} → 1+2 = 3, the minimum. Min cut = 3 = max flow found in the widgets above.

Refineries → terminals: replay the five augmenting paths Slides 19–34

Three refineries (1, 2, 3) ship gasoline through pumping stations (4, 5, 6) to two terminals (7, 8); a dummy source s and terminal t complete the max-flow network (slide 20). Labels show forward residual capacity near each arc's tail and backward (undo) capacity near its head. The slides pick five augmenting paths in turn — for each one, read the residuals along the highlighted path and click its bottleneck c*. After the last push, label the nodes still reachable from s to expose the minimum cut (slides 33–34).

total flow: 0

The dummy arcs get capacities "high enough" to never bind: s→1: 20, s→2: 80, s→3: 15 (each refinery's total outflow) and 7→t: 60, 8→t: 50 (each terminal's total inflow).
spoiler — open after the class has answered The five pushes: s-2-6-7-t (c*=50), s-2-5-6-8-t (20), s-1-4-5-8-t (20), s-3-5-8-t (10), s-2-4-7-t (10) — total 110, and no augmenting path remains (slide 32). Labelling from s reaches {s,1,2,3,4,5,6}; the arcs jumping to the unlabelled side — 4→7 (10), 6→7 (50), 6→8 (20), 5→8 (30) — form the minimum cut: 10+50+20+30 = 110 = max flow (slides 33–34).

Seattle → Boston — how many passengers can you fly? Slides 35–37

Every arc is a flight with limited seats; the Columbus–Charlotte leg may be flown twice, so its arc carries 2 × 11 = 22 (slide 35). Labels read passengers / seats. Click cities to route groups of passengers from Seattle to Boston — each completed route carries as many passengers as its fullest-booked leg allows, and backward (residual) hops let you re-route earlier groups. Beware: some flights land where no onward flight leaves…
