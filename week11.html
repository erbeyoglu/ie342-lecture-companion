How far is Adile Naşit from Brad Pitt? Slides 5–8

The Oracle of Bacon links any two film actors through shared movies — each shared movie is one step (one "degree"). The slides ask: how many steps does it take to connect Adile Naşit to Brad Pitt? Lock in a guess first, then reveal the connection one hop at a time.

Final check — the Oracle finds chains like this by solving a shortest path problem. On which graph does “degrees of separation” become a shortest path? (quiz item written for this demo)

Pick the graph where “3 steps” literally means “a shortest path with 3 edges”.

The Oracle of Bacon (University of Virginia) searches shared-movie data on roughly 350,000 people — it is literally solving a shortest path problem where nodes are actors and edges are shared films. Small-world claim from the slides: almost everyone on Earth is connected to everyone else within about 6 steps (rarely 7–8). Same idea in mathematics: the Erdős number — Paul Erdős (at least 1,525 papers) is 0, his co-authors are 1, their co-authors are 2 … The slides leave one question open for the class: what is the instructor's Erdős number?
spoiler — open after the class has answered 3 steps: Adile Naşit was in Aile Şerefi (1976) with Eriş Akman, who was in Slipstream (1989) with Ben Kingsley, who was in War Machine (2017) with Brad Pitt. And this is not the only 3-step link — unusual connections are not unusual at all.

Chain, path, or cycle? Slide 9

The 4-node directed network from the slide: arcs (1,2), (2,3), (3,4), (4,3) and (4,1). Each round highlights a sequence of arcs — classify it. Direction matters! The first three sequences are the slide's own examples; the last three are extra drills we added on the same figure.

sequence: — score: 0 / 0

Definitions from the slide: consecutive arcs sharing exactly one vertex form a chain; a chain where each arc's terminal node is the next arc's initial node is a path; a path that returns to its start is a cycle.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Slide 9's answers: (1,2)-(2,3)-(4,3) is a chain but not a path (the last arc runs backwards); (1,2)-(2,3)-(3,4) is a path; (1,2)-(2,3)-(3,4)-(4,1) is a cycle.

When should you trade in the car? Slides 11–14

A new car costs $12,000 at any time; maintenance climbs with age ($2k, $4k, $5k, $9k, $12k for ages 0–4) and the trade-in value falls ($7k, $6k, $2k, $1k, $0 for ages 1–5). Node i = start of year i; arc (i,j) = buy at year i, keep until year j; its cost is maintenance + $12k purchase − trade-in. Click nodes left to right to build a 5-year replacement plan from node 1 to node 6 — the cheapest plan is the shortest path. All costs in $1,000s. Dijkstra, step by step — you call the next node Slides 15–17

Dijkstra's algorithm on the car network: every node starts with tentative distance M (infinity) except the source at 0. Each round, the unvisited node with the smallest tentative label becomes permanent (★) and its unvisited neighbours are re-labelled. Click the node that becomes permanent next — the table fills exactly like slide 17.

Notation from the slide: * marks a permanent (final) label; the number in parentheses is the predecessor node that produced it; Min {old, via-current} shows each re-labelling.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Permanent order 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 with final labels 0, 7, 12, 19, 24, 31. Back-tracing the predecessors 31*(5) → 24*(3) → 12*(1) gives the shortest path 1-3-5-6 with length 31 — the same 31 the class found by hand in the game above.

Shelving books… as a shortest path? Slides 21–22

The library must shelve 200 4-inch, 100 8-inch and 80 12-inch books (each ½-inch thick). A shelf costs $2,300 to build, plus $5 per square inch of storage — and every book on a shelf occupies the shelf's height × its own thickness. Node i = "all books up to i inches are already shelved"; arc (i,j) = build one shelf of height j for every book taller than i inches. Step 1: compute the six arc costs and check them. Step 2: click the cheapest 0 → 12 route.

plan: 0 cost: $0

Cᵢⱼ = 2300 + (number of books taller than i and at most j inches) × 0.5 × j × 5. Book counts per range: 200 (≤4″), 300 (≤8″), 380 (≤12″), 100 (4–8″), 180 (4–12″), 80 (8–12″).
spoiler — open after the class has answered Slide 22: C₀,₄=4300, C₀,₈=8300, C₀,₁₂=13700, C₄,₈=4300, C₄,₁₂=7700, C₈,₁₂=4700. The shortest 0 → 12 path is 0–4–12 with length $12,000: build a 4-inch shelf for the 200 short books and a 12-inch shelf for the other 180.
