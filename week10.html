Can one price beat the lazy bound? Slides 4–7

Slide 4's knapsack: max 8x₁ + 9x₂ + 5x₃ + 4x₄ with 16x₁ + 20x₂ + 12x₃ + 10x₄ ≤ 42, x binary. Dropping the constraint bounds the optimum by 26 (slide 5). Instead, move the constraint into the objective with price λ ≥ 0 and solve the trivial relaxation LRλ. Slide λ (or drag on the chart) and hunt the λ that gives the lowest upper bound.

λ = 0.000 bound L(λ) = 26.00 your lowest: —

At λ = 0 the penalty is off — the bound is 26, exactly slide 5's ignore-the-constraint value.

L(λ) = 42λ + Σ max(0, cᵢ − λaᵢ): each item stays in only while its objective coefficient is positive, so the relaxation is solvable at sight for any λ — that is the whole point of Lagrangian relaxation (slide 3).
spoiler — open after the class has answered The curve is piecewise linear and dips to its minimum at λ★ = 5/12 ≈ 0.42, where L = 19.5 (slide 7): no knapsack solution can be worth more than 19.5 — far sharper than 26. Overshooting hurts: at λ = 1 every coefficient goes negative, all items drop out, and the bound balloons to 42 (slide 7).

You set the prices: multiplier adjustment, live Slides 9–17

Slide 10's problem has two relaxed constraints, so two multipliers. Slide 9's adjustment rule: start at λ = 0 with step k; after solving LRλ, raise the λ of every constraint the solution violates by k, lower it on positive slack, and halve k when the best bound stalls. Read the card, then predict the update for λ₁ and λ₂ and commit — the page follows the slides' exact iterations.

max 4x₁ + 5x₂ + 6x₃ + 7x₄   s.t.   c₁: 2x₁ + 2x₂ + 3x₃ + 4x₄ ≤ 7,   c₂: x₁ − x₂ + x₃ − x₄ ≤ 0,   x ∈ {0,1}⁴

Iteration 0: both prices are zero, so LR is the original objective over free binaries. Diagnose c₁ and c₂, then commit your update (k = 0.5).

The bound falls 22 → 20 → 18 → 16 while the relaxed solution keeps violating c₁; then it bounces, the step shrinks, and λ₂ finally wakes up.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Iteration 4 (λ₁ = 2) yields x = (0,1,0,0) — feasible for the original problem, so z = 5 becomes a lower bound (slide 15). The multipliers converge to λ★ = (1.83, 0.33) with bound 14.67 (slide 16), while implicit enumeration finds the true optimum x₃ = x₄ = 1 with z = 13 (slide 17): Lagrangian relaxation closed most of the 22 → 13 gap, but a duality gap of 1.67 remains.

Pack it better than the greedy heuristic Slides 18–20

Slide 18 asks how heuristics, Excel Solver and solvers like CBC compare — slide 19's knapsack is the test bed: max 13x₁ + 25x₂ + 19x₃ + 18x₄ + 27x₅ with 3x₁ + 3x₂ + 4x₃ + 4x₄ + 5x₅ ≤ 10. Greedy sorts by value/weight and packs whatever still fits — fast, no guarantee. Exact methods (branch & bound in Solver or CBC) certify optimality but cost more time and setup: that is slide 18's trade-off.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Greedy takes x₂ (ratio 8.33) then x₅ (5.40) and nothing else fits: value 52. The optimum swaps x₅ out for x₁ + x₃: pack {x₁, x₂, x₃}, weight exactly 10, value 57 — slide 20's table reads greedy 52 vs 57* from both Excel Solver and CBC.
