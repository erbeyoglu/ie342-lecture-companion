How few 60-second blocks? Slides 5–6

Example 18: commercials of 15, 16, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40 and 50 seconds must all fit into 60-second blocks — use as few blocks as possible. Click a commercial, then click a block to place it (click a placed commercial to take it back). Find the minimum before the IP on slide 6 appears.

Slide 6 asks: what is missing from the first formulation (min Σyj, capacity rows only)?

The capacity row 15x1j+16x2j+…+50x8j ≤ 60yj already links assignments to block usage — a block that receives anything must be opened.
spoiler — open after the class has answered Minimum = 5 blocks, e.g. {50}, {40, 16}, {35, 25}, {30, 20}, {15}. Why not 4? Total airtime is 231 s and 4 blocks give 240 s — only 9 s of slack. But the 50-s spot fits with nothing else (smallest partner is 15 s → 65 s > 60), so its block alone wastes 10 s. The missing constraint on slide 6 is Σj xij = 1: without it the IP happily leaves every commercial unassigned and "uses" zero blocks. (Minimum count computed by enumeration from the slide's data.)

    Does a legal cassette exist? Slides 13–14

    Example 22: place Madonna's 8 songs on two cassette sides. Each side must run 14–16 minutes, hold exactly two ballads, side 1 needs at least three hits, song 5 or 6 must be on side 1, songs 2 & 4 together on side 1 push song 5 to side 2, and song 1 on side 1 drags song 8 along. There is nothing to optimize — Min z = 0 — just find any assignment that passes every check.

      Make y₁₂ behave like x₁x₂ Slides 15–17

      Example 23 replaces the product x₁x₂ (and x₁² = x₁ for binaries) by a new binary y₁₂, turning min x₁ − x₁² + x₁x₂ + x₂ into min y₁₂ + x₂. Toggle the candidate constraints and watch the truth table: which set forces y₁₂ to equal x₁x₂ in all four rows?

      always on: y₁₂ ∈ {0, 1}
      Instancex₁x₂x₁·x₂allowed y₁₂verdict

      Slide 16's argument: y₁₂ ≤ x₁ and y₁₂ ≤ x₂ pin rows 1–3, but in row 4 a minimizing solver would gladly set y₁₂ = 0 — the cheap lie. The cut y₁₂ ≥ x₁ + x₂ − 1 closes it.
      spoiler — open after the class has answered All three constraints together (slide 17) leave exactly one legal y₁₂ in every row — the product is linearized no matter which way the objective pushes. With only y₁₂ ≥ x₁ + x₂ − 1 the table still shows slack in rows 1–3: a minimization happens to pick the right value there, but the upper bounds are what make y₁₂ = x₁x₂ an identity rather than a lucky outcome.

      Example 27: one machine, six jobs, precedence arrows 1→2, {1,2}→3, {2,4}→5, {4,5}→6. Every job pays its hourly cost for each hour until it completes: total penalty = Σ cjXj.       In the MIP (slides 31–32) the completion times Xj are continuous; immediate predecessors give Xj − Xk ≥ pj, and every unrelated pair needs an either-or disjunction — activated with big-M and a binary Zjk: Xj − Xk ≥ pj − MZjk and Xk − Xj ≥ pk − M(1 − Zjk).

The cheapest is the natural order 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6: completions 5, 11, 18, 28, 36, 50 h and total penalty 6·5 + 6·11 + 4·18 + 2·28 + 1·36 + 5·50 = $510. The worst legal order (4, 1, 2, 5, 6, 3) costs $680. (Computed by enumeration from the slide's table — the slide stops at the formulation.)
